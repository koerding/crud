\section{Discussion}

When reported adjusted associations are comparable to the crud scale, causal interpretation is fragile without additional design leverage, even if classical $p$-values are tiny. The crud-aware test provides a minimal magnitude standard for deciding when a small association is worth interpreting. As positive controls, smoking and lung cancer (RR$\approx 7$; converted to a Pearson correlation via Eq.~\eqref{eq:rr-to-r} of SI Appendix~\ref{app:scale-bridge} at representative population prevalences $p_X{=}0.20$, $p_Y{=}0.06$, this gives $r\approx 0.28$, or $\approx 3\sigma_K$ against NHANES at $K=10$) and within-facet HEXACO personality items (best pair $>$99.99th percentile) clear the threshold; SI Appendix~\ref{si:smoking-detail} gives the structural-ceiling caveat for binary correlations and the case-control-vs-cohort distinction. Crucially, $3\sigma_K$ alone is not what makes smoking--cancer compelling: decades of converging dose--response, cessation, mechanism, and animal evidence carry that case. The crud-aware test is a magnitude diagnostic, not a sole filter for causal credibility.

\paragraph{Scope and leverage.} Prior knowledge or design-based leverage effectively isolates variables from background dependence. Randomized treatments, discontinuities, policy changes, and valid instruments provide leverage that is orthogonal to generic correlation structure. Informed selection of which pairs to examine---from prior biology, mechanism, or pathway databases---narrows the empirical null; our impossibility result applies to the association statistic itself, not to the prior that selected the pair. Our results do not argue against such approaches; they target association-only workflows that interpret small adjusted pairwise correlations as causal evidence in the absence of additional leverage. The Bayes-error benchmark of Theorem~\ref{thm:assoc-only} (SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas}) bounds magnitude-only classification under a stylized mixture; it is not a claim about inference about $\rho_{ij}$ itself, which improves with $n$, nor about systems that exploit multivariate structure, nonlinear dependence, mechanistic priors, or interventions. The homogeneity of broad spectra and slow $\sigma_K$ shrinkage across our nine datasets is consistent with the obstacle generalizing beyond a single domain or modality; confirming truly cross-domain status requires a broader survey than nine cases can provide.

\paragraph{Empirical comparison to standard alternatives.} On a synthetic data-generating process with latent-factor confounding and a small set of embedded direct-edge pairs (SI Appendix~\ref{si:synthetic-benchmark}), the crud-aware diagnostic at $K=10$ recovers $96\%$ of direct edges at the conventional 95th-percentile threshold, against $48$ true positives and $947$ false positives ($30\times$ better precision than classical $|r|>1.96/\sqrt{n-1}$ and far better than BH-FDR, which fails to control its nominal FDR under structural confounding). The benchmark is a single stylized DGP rather than a method-dominance claim; in regimes with sparse rather than broadly-distributed confounding, BH-FDR will outperform. The takeaway is that the crud-aware diagnostic adds a useful magnitude check in the empirical regime the rest of this paper documents.

\paragraph{Relation to the causal-inference framework.} The crud-aware test does not replace identification arguments. The standard causal-inference framework---directed acyclic graphs, the do-calculus, instruments, regression discontinuities, sensitivity analyses for unobserved confounding \citep{pearl2009,imbens_rubin2015,cinelli2020,oster2019}---is the primary apparatus for translating observational data into causal claims. A small adjusted correlation can be perfectly informative if the DAG identifies the effect; a large one can be uninformative if confounding is severe. Our test is a magnitude diagnostic complementary to identification: it asks ``is the magnitude distinctive against same-pipeline residual dependence,'' which is a different question from ``is the effect identified.'' The test is most useful when identification is uncertain (the typical observational-claim setting), where it adds a magnitude floor on top of an unverified identification argument; it is least useful when identification is clean (e.g., a well-designed RCT), where magnitude alone is already meaningful relative to a sharp null.

\paragraph{Sensitivity of $\sigma_K$ to discretionary choices.} The reported quantitative multiples ($r/\sigma_K$, etc.) depend on $K$, preprocessing, design type, and reference-dataset choice. The qualitative conclusion---that the residual correlation distribution remains broadly distributed across these choices, and that small adjusted correlations rarely clear the conventional threshold against an approximately matched reference---is robust: $\sigma_K$ at $K=10$ and $K=50$ differ by less than a factor of two in seven of nine datasets (Table~\ref{tab:crud-scale}), and the alt-adjustment results (SI Appendix~\ref{app:alt-adjust}) confirm that Lasso does not collapse the residual distribution either. The quantitative pass/fail at the conventional threshold for a specific paper, however, is sensitive enough that we recommend reporting conclusions across a range of plausible $\sigma_K$ values rather than a single number (SI Appendix~\ref{si:how-to-use}, SI Appendix~\ref{si:reference-catalog}).

\paragraph{What to do when an effect does not clear the test.} Failing the test is not a verdict that the relation is causally unimportant; it is a verdict that the magnitude alone cannot carry the case. Constructive next steps are: (i) seek design leverage---randomization, instruments, regression discontinuities, negative controls---which breaks the generic-dependence problem the test diagnoses; (ii) pre-register the hypothesis and replicate in an independent dataset, which is the most reliable correction for selection-driven inflation; (iii) accept that converging evidence (mechanism, dose--response, removal effect, prior literature) can carry a causal claim even when magnitude alone does not, as in the smoking--cancer case. When none of these are available and the magnitude is in the bulk of the empirical null, the right reporting standard is to label the association as not distinctive against background and treat any causal interpretation as provisional.

\paragraph{Limitations.} All results concern linear dependence (correlations, linear PC adjustment, covariance spectra). In domains with substantial nonlinear dependence---gene regulatory networks, neural population codes, epidemiological dose--response---$\sigma_K$ is a lower bound on the true background dependence; whether nonlinear adjustment would shrink the nonlinear background faster than linear adjustment shrinks the linear background is not tested here. The quantitative $\sigma_K$ varies across domains and preprocessing; we use PC regression as a deliberately generic adjustment, not a claim of causal identification. In high-dimensional regimes ($p$ not small relative to $n$), estimating dominant directions adds uncertainty to $\sigma_K$ that the test does not propagate (SI Appendix~\ref{app:alt-adjust}). The decision-theoretic limit is per-pair, equal-variance, Gaussian, and fixed-$K$. SI Appendix~\ref{si:practical-stakes} discusses asymmetric costs of skepticism in concrete settings (e.g., NIH R01 marginal-grant analysis). Three foundational issues are out of scope here and remain open: an analytic correction for selected target pairs (we recommend pre-registration and stratified references but provide no closed-form fix); whether the empirical null reflects latent dependence or unmodeled adjustment (we proceed under the practical position that magnitude typical of same-pipeline pairs is not by itself causal evidence, regardless of which interpretation prevails); and broader robustness of the impossibility theorem beyond the stylized Gaussian-mixture model.
