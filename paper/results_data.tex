\section{Results}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets-main}

We analyze nine large datasets spanning neuroscience, genomics, psychology, political science, vision, and population health (Figure~\ref{fig:datasets}; Methods; SI~\ref{si:datasets} gives full descriptions and provenance): NHANES, precinct-level 2016 voting and Census demographics, GTEx v8 RNA-seq, Allen Institute mouse brain RNA-seq, HEXACO, CIFAR-10, Kay fMRI, Haxby fMRI, and Stringer mouse V1. The nine span dimensionalities ($p$ from 83 to 6{,}000), sample sizes ($n$ from $\sim$8{,}000 to $\sim$30{,}000), and measurement types (continuous neural signals, binary/ordinal survey items, count-based expression, pixel intensities), so patterns common to all cannot easily be attributed to a single data modality. This is a convenience sample of large public datasets; generalization beyond the nine remains an empirical question.

\subsection{Empirical finding: broad correlations persist after adjustment}

For uniformly sampled feature pairs, we observe a broad spread of correlations (Figure~\ref{fig:corr-dist}, left; Table~\ref{tab:crud-scale}). Given the large sample sizes, classical iid correlation tests label most of these correlations as nominally significant, even though most random pairs are implausible candidates for direct causal relations. After PC regression at $K=10$ (Methods; sensitivity across $K$ in Table~\ref{tab:crud-scale}), the residual correlation distribution remains wide (Figure~\ref{fig:corr-dist}, right). The core empirical fact: the background correlation distribution is wide even after generic adjustment, so ``significant'' does not mean ``distinctive.'' This is not a multiple-testing point: even ignoring $p$-values, the typical residual correlation scale after generic adjustment is substantial, so small adjusted associations are not distinctive evidence in the first place. Across the low-rank adjustments we tested (PC regression and Lasso, Appendix~\ref{app:alt-adjust}), none collapsed the residual distribution on any of the nine datasets.

To give these numbers a concrete interpretation, for $r=0.10$ to clear the conventional 95th-percentile crud-aware threshold ($z_{\mathrm{crud}}>1.96$, where $z_{\mathrm{crud}}=|r|/\sqrt{\sigma_K^2+1/(n-1)}$) the crud scale must satisfy $\sigma_K\lesssim 0.05$. At $K=0$ this holds in only two of nine datasets (Stringer, GTEx); after removing ten PCs, only three (Stringer, Kay, HEXACO). In the remaining six datasets, $r=0.10$ does not clear the threshold at $K=10$ within the fixed-$K$ model, regardless of $n$.

\subsection{Mechanism: power-law spectra}

Why does removing broad shared variation not shrink the correlation distribution faster? Across all nine datasets, explained variance decays approximately as a power law (Figure~\ref{fig:spectra}): the spectrum is roughly linear on log--log axes, meaning shared variation is distributed across many components rather than concentrated in the first few. This contrasts with the common implicit ``spike'' assumption behind low-rank adjustment: if confounding variance were concentrated in a few dominant components, removing them would collapse the background. Instead, removing the top $K$ PCs eliminates only a modest portion of the dependence structure unless $K$ is large, which is why residual correlations remain broadly distributed even at $K=10$. Choosing a large $K$ is not a remedy: PCs beyond the leading ones are estimated with increasing noise, so removing too many replaces bias with variance (visible for Precinct at $K=50$). We treat the approximately power-law spectrum as an empirical regularity; mechanism is outside scope. Robustness checks against artifactual explanations are in Appendix~\ref{app:robustness}.
