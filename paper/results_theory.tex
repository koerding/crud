\subsection{Bridge lemma: the crud scale and its decay}

We formalize the link from the eigenvalue spectrum to the residual correlation distribution. Theorem~\ref{thm:crud-scale} (SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas}) shows that under a Haar-like delocalization condition on the eigenvectors of the covariance, the SD of the residual correlation $\rho^{(K)}_{ij}$ across uniformly random pairs is well approximated by a ratio of eigenvalue tail sums:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigma-K}
\sigma_K \;:=\; \frac{\sqrt{\sum_{k>K}\lambda_k^2}}{\sum_{k>K}\lambda_k}.
\end{equation}
The numerator measures how unevenly the remaining variance is distributed across components; the denominator sets the scale. Important: Table~\ref{tab:crud-scale} reports the empirical residual-correlation SD measured directly across uniformly sampled feature pairs, not the closed-form prediction~\eqref{eq:sigma-K}. The downstream crud-aware test calibrations therefore rely on direct measurement, not on the spectral formula. The formula's accuracy as a \emph{predictor} of the empirical SD varies across datasets: SI Appendix~\ref{app:assumption-audit} (Test~3) reports predicted-vs-empirical correlations $r>0.94$ in seven of nine datasets, with weaker agreement in CIFAR-10 ($r=0.88$) and RNA-Seq ($r=0.64$), and Test~4 reports diagonal concentration that holds well in high-$p$ data and degrades in low-$p$ settings (CV up to $1.13$ in Precinct, $0.81$ in NHANES at $K=50$). Eigenvector delocalization is also met only approximately ($3.8$--$11.2\times$ the Haar ideal; Test~2). The resulting bias on the spectral formula is conservative for the crud-scale claim where it applies (localization tends to make~\eqref{eq:sigma-K} overestimate the typical residual correlation; SI Appendix~\ref{app:approx-robust}); where it does not apply quantitatively (low-$p$, heavy-tailed regimes), the empirical $\sigma_K$ in Table~\ref{tab:crud-scale} stands on its own as a direct observation.

If the eigenvalues follow a power law $\lambda_k \approx c k^{-\alpha}$, then $\sigma_K$ decays slowly: in the especially relevant $\alpha \approx 1$ case, $\sigma_K \approx 1/(\sqrt{K}\log(p/K))$ (Corollary~\ref{cor:powerlaw}, SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas}). Removing $K=10$ components can leave a substantial residual crud scale when $p$ is large, formalizing why low-rank adjustment shrinks the correlation distribution only modestly.

\paragraph{$\sigma_K$ as a pipeline functional.} $\sigma_K$ is a population functional of the joint $(\Sigma, K, \text{preprocessing pipeline})$, not of the data alone. Estimating it requires a fixed pipeline (z-scoring policy, missing-data handling, PCA implementation, sub-sampling); changing any of these changes $\sigma_K$. Cross-paper or cross-domain $\sigma_K$ comparisons therefore require pipeline matching, not just dataset matching, and are best interpreted within a single fixed pipeline (SI Appendix~\ref{si:reference-catalog}).

\paragraph{Asymptotic regime.} Different theorems hold different quantities fixed: Theorem~\ref{thm:crud-scale} is a $p\to\infty$ statement at fixed or slowly growing $K$; Theorem~\ref{thm:assoc-only} (decision-theoretic) holds $\sigma_K$ fixed; Theorem~\ref{thm:parametric-crud} (parametric crud-aware test) holds $p$ fixed and varies $n$. The ``regardless of sample size'' claim in the abstract refers specifically to the fixed-$(p,K)$ regime of Theorem~\ref{thm:parametric-crud}: the parametric variance does not vanish with $n$ when $\sigma_K^2$ is the dominant term. We do not claim joint uniformity across $(n,p,K)$; statements at different limits should be read independently.

\subsection{Decision-theoretic limit}

A complementary question: how well can magnitude alone classify a pair as causally linked versus correlated by background dependence? Theorem~\ref{thm:assoc-only} (SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas}) gives the Bayes-error benchmark for this magnitude-only problem. Under a stylized Gaussian mixture in which a direct causal edge induces a mean shift $\mu$ in the adjusted association statistic $T_{ij}$, the minimum achievable misclassification error for any rule using only $T_{ij}$ is $\Phi(-|\mu|/(2\sigma_K))$. When $|\mu|\lesssim \sigma_K$, this rate is a constant bounded away from zero. Two clarifications matter. First, this is the irreducibility of magnitude-only classification under this mixture, not a claim about what inference can recover about $\rho_{ij}$ itself: an analyst with a sample correlation $\hat\rho_{ij}$ estimates $\rho_{ij}$ ever more precisely as $n\to\infty$. The benchmark reflects overlap between the two classes in the mixture, not an obstruction to estimation. Second, whether the model-level $\sigma_K$ matches the conceptually natural ``background dependence'' in a given domain depends on whether the chosen adjustment captures that domain's confounding structure (SI Appendix~\ref{si:test-details}). Sparse causal structure ($\pi \ll 1$) further compounds the magnitude-only limitation by collapsing positive predictive value (SI Appendix~\ref{si:base-rate}). Heavy-tailed residuals, additional variance structure, and selected target pairs are discussed in SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas} and SI Appendix~\ref{si:test-details}.
