\section*{Significance Statement}

Observational science routinely interprets small adjusted correlations as evidence for causal effects. We test this assumption in nine large datasets and find that most pairs of measured variables are correlated to a non-trivial degree, even after standard statistical adjustments. As a consequence, many associations that look ``significant'' by classical standards are not distinctive: their magnitudes are typical of randomly chosen variable pairs in the same dataset. We propose a diagnostic---a crud-aware test---that benchmarks any reported correlation against the empirical null of same-pipeline residuals. Only associations that clearly exceed this background should be treated as evidence for a direct causal relationship; small adjusted correlations should be reported with the caveat that their magnitude alone is not a basis for causal claims.

\begin{abstract}
Causal claims in observational research typically start with two measured variables, adjust for confounders, and interpret the remaining correlation as evidence of a causal effect. For this logic to work, adjusted correlations must be informative about their underlying causal effects. We test this assumption using nine large datasets spanning health, neuroscience, psychology, genomics, politics, and vision. Across these datasets, most pairwise correlations are classically significant---the so-called ``crud'' background. We trace this background to approximately $1/f$ eigenvalue spectra, which distribute shared variance across many directions so that the residual crud scale $\sigma_K$ shrinks only slowly with the number of removed components: within the fixed-$K$ regime we model, the moderate-dimensional adjustments we tested (PC regression and Lasso) do not reduce $\sigma_K$ to negligible levels in any of the nine datasets, regardless of sample size. A decision-theoretic result formalizes the consequence: when the causal signal is comparable to $\sigma_K$, no rule that compresses each pair to a one-dimensional adjusted-association statistic can reliably separate signal from noise. We propose a crud-aware $z$-test that benchmarks observed correlations against the domain's empirical null. In most domains studied here, even a correlation as large as $r=0.10$ does not clear the crud-aware threshold.
\end{abstract}
