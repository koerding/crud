\section*{Significance Statement}

Much of observational science follows a common recipe: measure two quantities of interest, statistically adjust for confounders, and interpret the remaining correlation as evidence of a causal link. This logic assumes that adjusted correlations are informative about causation. We test this assumption across nine large datasets from health, neuroscience, psychology, genomics, politics, and vision, and find that nearly every pair of measured features is significantly correlated---the so-called ``crud'' background. These correlations are real, not artifacts of multiple testing: they arise because shared variance in real-world data spreads across many dimensions, so standard statistical adjustments cannot remove the background. Many small associations reported as significant are indistinguishable from this background, even after controlling for confounders, and no increase in sample size resolves the problem. We propose a practical diagnostic---a crud-aware test---that benchmarks any reported correlation against the domain background. Only associations that clearly exceed this background should be treated as evidence for a direct causal relationship.

\begin{abstract}
Causal claims in observational research typically start with two measured variables (say education and income), adjust for confounders (say parental socioeconomic status), and interpret the remaining correlation as evidence of a causal effect. For this logic to work, adjusted correlations must be informative about their underlying causal effects. We test this assumption using nine large datasets spanning health, neuroscience, psychology, genomics, politics, and vision. Across these datasets, most pairwise correlations are classically significant---the so-called ``crud'' background. We trace this background to approximately $1/f$ eigenvalue spectra, which distribute shared variance across many directions so that the residual crud scale $\sigma_K$ shrinks only slowly with the number of removed components: no moderate-dimensional correction can eliminate background dependence, regardless of sample size. A decision-theoretic result formalizes the consequence: when the causal signal is comparable to $\sigma_K$, no association-only rule can reliably separate signal from noise. We propose a crud-aware $z$-test that benchmarks observed correlations against the domain background; associations that are highly significant by classical standards can become entirely unremarkable once background dependence is accounted for.
\end{abstract}
