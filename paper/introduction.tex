\section{Introduction}

Across biomedicine, social science, and behavioral research, causal claims increasingly rely on observational associations in large datasets. Randomized experiments remain the benchmark for counterfactual effects, but intervention is often infeasible, so small adjusted associations are routinely interpreted as evidence about causal mechanism. For this logic to work, adjusted correlations must be informative about their underlying causal effects. We test that assumption by asking how informative those associations remain in the presence of the background dependence structure typical of modern datasets. We focus on small-effect causal claims that depend on adjusted associations standing out from background dependence; large effects easily clear any reasonable background, and prediction tasks do not require causal interpretation.

Related work emphasizes that widespread weak dependence is common: Meehl's ``crud factor'' \citep{meehl1990} argued that in large behavioral datasets, most pairs of variables are non-trivially correlated; \citet{gwernEverything} reviews the ``everything is correlated'' perspective. Sensitivity analyses \citep{cinelli2020,oster2019} ask the hypothetical question of how strong unobserved confounding would need to be to nullify a result; we ask the empirical question of what background dependence actually looks like in a domain. Approximate $1/f$ spectral structure has been documented across many complex systems \citep{press1978}. Our contribution connects these threads: we quantify the background correlation distribution across domains, link its slow shrinkage under generic adjustment to eigenvalue spectra, and propose a crud-aware test for interpreting small adjusted associations. A complementary literature documents that small published effects are often inflated \citep{ioannidis2005,gelman2014,osc2015}; SI Appendix~\ref{app:overclaim} surveys this evidence field by field and shows that the typical published effect would not clear our test in observational medicine, nutrition, common-variant genetics, or BWAS-style neuroscience.

A concrete example: a recent study reported a nominally significant association between folate intake and mortality in adults with type-2 diabetes ($r \approx 0.02$, $p_{\mathrm{classical}} < 0.05$, $n \approx 8{,}000$) \citep{liu2022}. Compared to the background distribution of correlations among NHANES variables, however, it falls in the middle: its crud-aware percentile rank is $0.19$ (i.e., its magnitude is exceeded by $81\%$ of same-pipeline reference pairs; Section~\ref{sec:worked-examples}). The classical test detects that the correlation is nonzero; the percentile rank shows it is not distinctive against the background.

We operationalize the background dependence scale, for this paper, as the residual correlation standard deviation across uniformly sampled feature pairs after a fixed adjustment (regression on the top $K$ principal components), and use it as the benchmark for whether an adjusted association is surprising. Whether this matches a domain's conceptual notion of background depends on whether the adjustment captures the domain's confounding structure. The empirical null is not a ``no direct edge'' null---in real domains, pervasive confounding ensures that uniformly sampled pairs share indirect dependence---it is the distribution of typical same-pipeline residual correlations. The argument is correspondingly weaker: a target sitting in the bulk cannot be cleanly read as evidence of a direct causal relation, because the same residual magnitude can arise from typical residual confounding alone. Across our nine domains, most small adjusted associations do not separate from the empirical null. We (i) document this empirically, (ii) trace it to approximately power-law eigenvalue spectra, (iii) prove a Bayes-error benchmark (Theorems~\ref{thm:crud-scale}--\ref{thm:assoc-only} in SI Appendix~\ref{app:bridge-lemmas}) for magnitude-only classification under a stylized mixture, and (iv) propose a crud-aware test against the empirical null.
