\section{Discussion}

When reported adjusted associations are comparable to the crud scale, causal interpretation is fragile without additional design leverage, even if classical $p$-values are tiny. The crud-aware test provides a minimal magnitude standard for deciding when a small association is worth interpreting. As positive controls, smoking and lung cancer (RR$\approx 7$, converted to a Pearson correlation via Eq.~\eqref{eq:rr-to-r} of SI Appendix~\ref{app:scale-bridge} at representative population prevalences gives $r\approx 0.28$, or $\approx 3\sigma_K$ against NHANES at $K=10$) and within-facet HEXACO personality items (best pair $>$99.99th percentile) clear the threshold; SI Appendix~\ref{si:smoking-detail} gives the structural-ceiling caveat for binary correlations and the case-control-vs-cohort distinction.

\paragraph{Scope and leverage.} Prior knowledge or design-based leverage---randomization, discontinuities, valid instruments, mechanistic priors---effectively isolates variables from background dependence and is orthogonal to generic correlation structure. Our results target association-only workflows that interpret small adjusted pairwise correlations as causal evidence in the absence of additional leverage. The crud-aware test is a magnitude diagnostic complementary to identification (SI Appendix~\ref{si:causal-inference-framework}); it asks ``is the magnitude distinctive against same-pipeline residual dependence,'' not ``is the effect identified.'' The test is most useful when identification is uncertain, where it adds a magnitude floor on top of an unverified identification argument; it is least useful when identification is clean (e.g., a well-designed RCT), where magnitude alone is already meaningful relative to a sharp null.

\paragraph{Empirical comparison to standard alternatives.} On a synthetic data-generating process with latent-factor confounding and a small set of embedded direct-edge pairs (SI Appendix~\ref{si:synthetic-benchmark}), averaged over $50$ seeds, the crud-aware diagnostic at $K=10$ recovers $98\%$ of direct edges at the conventional 95th-percentile rank with precision $\approx 0.05$ ($30\times$ better than classical), and recovers $90\%$ at the 99th percentile with F1 $\approx 0.36$. This dominates classical $|r|>1.96/\sqrt{n-1}$, BH-FDR (theoretical and dependent-BY variants), and Efron's empirical-null + BH \citep{efron2004} on every metric: BY-FDR and Efron+BH improve modestly over classical BH but still fail to control nominal FDR because they only recalibrate the marginal distribution of test statistics, not the structural confounding that PC adjustment removes.

\paragraph{Real small effects supported by RCT/mechanism would also fail this test.} Several real, clinically important interventions land at small RR's that the magnitude-only test would not clear: aspirin in primary cardiovascular prevention (pooled $\mathrm{RR}\approx 0.94$ for CV mortality), low-dose statins ($\mathrm{RR}\approx 0.78$ for major CV events), ACE inhibitors in non-diabetic CKD progression. This is not a failure of the test---it is the correct diagnosis: \emph{the magnitude alone is not what makes these effects compelling}. What carries each case is RCT replication, mechanism, dose-response, removal effect, and biological plausibility---design leverage on top of small magnitudes. The test correctly diagnoses small-RR clinical effects as not magnitude-distinctive, which is consistent with their being supported by other lines of evidence rather than by raw correlation.

The mirror-image objection---``my mechanism privileges this pair, so the random-pair null is the wrong reference''---is addressed in SI Appendix~\ref{si:random-pair-null}: in practice, ``I had a mechanism'' is often a post-hoc rationalization of one story among many, and the magnitude distribution of mechanism-selected pairs in the literature is much closer to the random-pair distribution than introspection suggests. $\sigma_K$ is itself a pipeline-relative quantity (SI Appendix~\ref{si:sigma-K-sensitivity}); cross-paper comparisons require pipeline matching.

\paragraph{What to do when an effect does not clear the test.} Failing the test is not a verdict that the relation is causally unimportant; it is a verdict that the magnitude alone cannot carry the case. Constructive next steps are: (i) seek design leverage---randomization, instruments, regression discontinuities, negative controls; (ii) pre-register the hypothesis and replicate in an independent dataset; (iii) accept that converging evidence (mechanism, dose--response, removal effect, prior literature) can carry a causal claim even when magnitude alone does not, as in the smoking--cancer case. For fields where design leverage is structurally unavailable---nutritional, environmental, and lifestyle epidemiology---SI Appendix~\ref{si:fallback-advice} gives a magnitude-as-descriptor fallback.

\paragraph{Limitations.} All results concern linear dependence (correlations, linear PC adjustment, covariance spectra); $\sigma_K$ is a lower bound on background dependence in domains with substantial nonlinear structure. The quantitative $\sigma_K$ varies across domains and preprocessing; the decision-theoretic limit is per-pair, equal-variance, Gaussian, and fixed-$K$. Three foundational issues are out of scope here and remain open: an analytic correction for selected target pairs (we recommend pre-registration and stratified references but provide no closed-form fix); whether the empirical null reflects latent dependence or unmodeled adjustment (left open by the test, which is calibrated for the magnitude question regardless of which interpretation prevails); and broader robustness of the impossibility theorem beyond the stylized Gaussian-mixture model.
